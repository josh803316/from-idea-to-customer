## The indispensable tool

Abstraction is how humans manage complexity.

You can't hold an entire distributed system in your head. You can't hold an entire organization, a full product surface, or a year-long roadmap in your head. So you compress. You create layers. You name things. You build interfaces that hide the details of what's underneath so that you can operate at a higher level without re-deriving everything from first principles every time.

This is not a limitation. This is the whole game. The history of software engineering is largely the history of better abstractions — functions over raw machine instructions, high-level languages over assembly, frameworks over low-level libraries, managed services over self-hosted infrastructure. Each layer compresses complexity into a shape that can be managed, built upon, and communicated across teams.

Without abstraction, there is no scale. Every abstraction you use — every framework, every API, every organizational process — is compressing something real that somebody had to understand so you don't have to.

## The dangerous part

Abstraction is indifferent to what it's compressing.

It will just as happily package a bad practice, a broken process, or a hack into a clean-looking shape as it will a sound one. And once it's abstracted, **it looks the same as everything else.**

A well-named function that wraps elegant logic and a well-named function that wraps a terrible workaround are indistinguishable from the outside. A clean-looking CI/CD pipeline and a clean-looking CI/CD pipeline that's silently papering over a fundamental architectural flaw both have green checkmarks.

This is the deep problem with abstraction: the quality of what's underneath is not visible from the surface. And over time, the people who knew what was underneath leave, and the people who inherit the abstraction treat the surface as the reality.

<Aside type="warning">
The most dangerous abstractions in any organization are the ones that have been in place so long that no one remembers they *are* abstractions. They've become "just how we do things" — which is the point where a compression of a real concept becomes indistinguishable from dogma.
</Aside>

## Abstractions beyond code

Engineers tend to think of abstraction as a purely technical concept. It isn't.

Every organizational structure is an abstraction — a compression of "how we coordinate" into a shape that can be understood and operated on. Job titles are abstractions of responsibility. Meeting cadences are abstractions of collaboration. Roadmap processes are abstractions of prioritization. Hiring rubrics are abstractions of "what good looks like."

Each one can be packaging something healthy or something broken. A hiring rubric that was designed thoughtfully five years ago might now be compressing an outdated understanding of the role — but it still *looks* like a rigorous hiring process. A meeting cadence that was designed to solve a specific coordination problem might still be running long after the coordination problem was solved, because nobody asked what it was abstracting.

The question applies everywhere: **what is this actually compressing?** Is the underlying concept sound, or did someone just make a bad practice look presentable? Is this abstraction adding real value, or is it hiding a problem that will eventually surface in a more damaging way?

## The recursive question

The ownership mindset demands that you dig beneath abstractions rather than just consume them.

Not all the way down, all the time — that's not operationally feasible. You use abstractions because they let you operate at scale. But for the squares you own, you need to know what's underneath.

The practice: for each abstraction you rely on in a square you own, ask once:

*What is this actually compressing? Is the underlying concept sound?*

If the answer is "I don't know what's underneath," that's the gap to close. Not necessarily by fixing what's underneath — sometimes the right answer is understanding what's there and making a conscious decision to continue wrapping it. But the decision has to be conscious.

The recursive version: when the answer reveals another abstraction, ask the question again. Keep going until you hit either bedrock (something you understand at a first-principles level) or until you hit "I don't know" (which tells you exactly where the ownership gap is).

<Aside type="insight">
**Pattern to watch for:** When someone asks "why do we do it this way?" and the answer is "that's just how we do it" — you've found an abstraction that has been mistaken for bedrock. This is where the most valuable questions live, and it's where the least experienced people are least likely to ask them, because they don't yet know that "that's just how we do it" is not an explanation.
</Aside>

## Exercise

Choose one abstraction you use daily — a library, a process, a framework, a team ritual.

Apply the recursive question once:
1. What is this actually compressing? What was happening before this abstraction existed?
2. Is the underlying concept sound?
3. Is this abstraction adding genuine value, or is it hiding a problem?

Then go one level deeper: whatever you found underneath, apply the question again. Where do you reach bedrock? Where do you reach "I don't know"?

Conference room exercise: explain what you discovered to the LLM playing a new engineer joining your team tomorrow. Assume they will use this abstraction on day one. What do they need to understand about what's underneath — and what can they safely treat as a black box?
