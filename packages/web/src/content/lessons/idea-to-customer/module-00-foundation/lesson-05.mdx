## The most common default

The default relationship most people have with constraints is acceptance.

"That's just how it works." "We can't change that." "It's always been this way." "Legal won't allow it." "The database can't handle that load."

This posture — treating constraints as walls — is so common that most people don't even notice they're doing it. The constraint becomes invisible, absorbed into "reality" rather than recognized as a specific, nameable condition that someone chose or that emerged from decisions that can be revisited.

Ownership requires a fundamentally different posture. Constraints are not walls. They are material to be worked with.

## The first skill: identification

Before you can do anything with a constraint, you have to be able to name it.

This sounds trivial. It isn't.

Most people can't name the constraints they're operating under because they've internalized them as features of the environment rather than recognizing them as specific conditions. The constraint is invisible because it's never been separated from the background.

The discipline of constraint identification is: look at what you can't do, and ask why. Not "why can't I do it" as a rhetorical expression of frustration — why, as a genuine inquiry. What is the constraint? Where did it come from? Who made the decision that created it? What were the conditions at the time? Do those conditions still hold?

<Aside type="insight">
The most important constraints to identify are the ones that are no longer constraints but haven't been reclassified yet. They look like rules, but they're artifacts of decisions made under conditions that no longer apply. "We can't do that" — but the reason we can't do that stopped being true two years ago.
</Aside>

## The four strategies

Once you can name a constraint, you have four options. The skill is knowing which strategy fits which constraint.

### 1. Remove it

Is this constraint actually necessary, or is it an artifact of a decision that no longer applies?

Some constraints simply shouldn't exist anymore. The technology changed. The team changed. The market changed. The original reason for the constraint is gone, but the constraint is still treated as permanent because nobody questioned it.

The practice: for every constraint you identify, ask "why does this exist?" at least twice. If the answer to the second "why" is "I don't know" or "historical reasons," you have a constraint worth examining for removal.

### 2. Reshape it

You can't eliminate the constraint, but can you redefine it?

A deadline you can't move might be met by changing the scope. A technical limitation might be sidestepped by reframing the problem. A resource constraint might be manageable if you restructure what the resource is doing.

Reshaping is the most creative of the four strategies because it requires finding the actual shape of the constraint — not the stated version of it, but the underlying reality. "We have a hard deadline of April 15" is a statement. The constraint underneath might be "the customer needs to demo this at their quarterly board meeting on April 15." If you can find an alternative way to satisfy the board meeting requirement, you've reshaped the constraint.

### 3. Manage and mitigate

Some constraints are real and immovable. The skill is building around them — reducing their impact, creating buffers, designing systems that account for them rather than pretending they don't exist.

This is not the same as accepting the constraint passively. Managing a constraint is active: you're aware of it, you've chosen not to fight it, and you've designed your approach to minimize its impact.

The error is treating constraints as invisible and discovering their impact through failure rather than through planning.

### 4. Wrap it

Sometimes you can't fix the underlying issue, so you build a layer that contains it. This is a legitimate strategy — but only when you *know* you're doing it and why.

Wrapping a constraint is like treating a symptom rather than a root cause. Sometimes that's the right call — you need to manage the symptom now while the root cause is addressed separately, or the root cause is outside your control. But the wrapping has to be conscious.

The danger is when wrapping becomes invisible. Everyone forgets there's a problem underneath. The wrapper becomes "the process," and the actual constraint lives beneath it, accumulating risk. Every automated process that patches over a systemic problem is a constraint that's been wrapped rather than addressed — and over time, when the wrapper eventually fails, nobody knows what it was actually wrapping.

<Aside type="warning">
Wrapping is how technical debt hides. It's also how organizational dysfunction hides. The constraint was real. Someone built something to manage it. The builder left. The new people inherited the wrapper and forgot what it was wrapping. Now the wrapper is "how we do things" and the constraint underneath keeps getting worse with nobody noticing.
</Aside>

## Constraints by hat

Each hat encounters different constraints and brings different strategies.

**PM:** Market timing, customer expectations, roadmap commitments. The PM's constraint work is often about managing commitments — the commitment was made before the constraint was fully understood, and now the job is reshaping or managing.

**Marketer:** Channel constraints, budget constraints, message clarity constraints. Reshaping is common — the budget can't grow, but the allocation can be redesigned. Message constraints often get removed when the team discovers they were writing for an audience that doesn't exist.

**Sales Lead:** Procurement cycles, security review timelines, legal approval. Almost all management and mitigation — these constraints are external and largely non-negotiable. The skill is designing the sales process to account for them.

**CTO:** Technical debt, infrastructure limits, team capability. All four strategies apply. Technical constraints get removed (refactored), reshaped (worked around), managed (acceptance criteria that account for the limitation), and wrapped (the intermediate layer while the real fix is built).

## Exercise

Identify one constraint in your current work that you've been treating as a wall.

1. Name it precisely — not "we're slow" but "the deployment process takes 45 minutes and it blocks the team every afternoon."
2. Trace its origin. When was this constraint created? By whom? Under what conditions?
3. Apply each of the four strategies to it as a thought exercise. Could you remove it? Reshape it? Manage it more actively? Wrap it as an intermediate step?
4. Which strategy is actually right, given your current situation and constraints?

Then: bring this constraint to the conference room exercise. Present the constraint and your proposed strategy to the LLM playing your CTO. Get them to challenge your reasoning. The CTO hat is excellent for pressure-testing constraint strategies because it has to weigh technical reality, business timelines, and team capacity simultaneously.
