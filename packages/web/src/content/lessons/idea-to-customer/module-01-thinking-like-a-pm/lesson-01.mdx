## The biggest mistake engineers make

Engineers love solutions. Give us a problem and we immediately start designing the architecture, picking the stack, naming the tables. It feels productive — because it is. Building is what we're good at.

But there's a step before building that most engineers skip: **deeply understanding the problem**.

A feature built for the wrong problem is waste. Worse, it's *confident* waste — you ship something polished that nobody asked for.

<Aside type="insight">
**The PM rule of thumb**: Spend at least as long understanding the problem as you spend designing the solution. Most engineers get this ratio backwards.
</Aside>

## What a user problem actually looks like

A user problem is not:
- "We need a dashboard"
- "Users want dark mode"
- "We should add notifications"

These are solutions wearing problem-shaped clothing.

A real user problem sounds like:
> *"I lose track of where our project stands between weekly standups, so I'm always surprised on Fridays."*

Notice what's in that sentence:
1. A **person** ("I")
2. A **situation** ("between weekly standups")
3. A **pain** ("lose track", "surprised on Fridays")

No solution mentioned. No technology implied. Just a human struggling with something.

## The Job to Be Done framework

Clayton Christensen's "Jobs to Be Done" (JTBD) framework is the most useful PM tool you'll learn this week. The core idea: people don't buy products; they *hire* products to do a job.

The format:

> When I **[situation]**, I want to **[motivation]**, so I can **[expected outcome]**.

Applied to our example:
> When I'm preparing for Friday standup, I want to quickly see what changed this week, so I can give an accurate status without hunting through Slack and Jira.

This one sentence contains everything a PM needs to write a spec and everything an engineer needs to build the right thing.

## The three questions

Before writing a single line of code or a single line of spec, a PM must answer:

**1. Who has this problem?**
Be specific. "All users" is not an answer. "Engineering managers at companies with 10–50 engineers who ship weekly" is an answer.

**2. How painful is it?**
Pain has a spectrum. Is this a "nice to have" or a "I have a workaround that takes 2 hours every Friday"? The more painful, the more people will pay for a solution.

**3. Why haven't they already solved it?**
This is the sneaky one. If the problem is real and painful, why is nobody else solving it? Understanding the *why* reveals the real constraint — which is often what your product needs to address.

## Exercise

Pick a workflow in your current job that feels inefficient. Write:

1. The JTBD statement
2. Answers to all three PM questions

Don't use the words "dashboard", "notification", or "integration" in your answers. If you find yourself wanting to, you're describing a solution, not a problem.
