## Why one page?

A 20-page PRD is a failure of prioritization. If a PM can't explain what they want in one page, they don't know what they want yet.

The one-page constraint forces ruthlessness. Every sentence has to earn its place.

<Aside type="warning">
Long PRDs feel thorough but they create a false sense of alignment. Engineers read the first two sections and assume they understand the rest. Keep it short enough that there's nowhere to hide.
</Aside>

## The FITC one-page PRD template

```
# [Feature Name]

## Problem
[One paragraph. JTBD statement. Who, situation, pain.]

## Why now
[One paragraph. Why is this the right moment to build this?]

## Success metrics
[2–3 measurable outcomes. How will we know it worked?]

## Scope
### In scope
- [Bullet list of what we're building]

### Out of scope
- [Bullet list of what we're explicitly NOT building]

## Open questions
- [Things we don't know yet that could change the design]
```

That's it. Every field is mandatory. None of the sections can be skipped.

## Walking through each section

### Problem
You wrote this in Lesson 1. Drop it in. If it's longer than 3 sentences, trim it.

### Why now
This is where most PRDs fall short. "Because users asked for it" is not a reason. Good answers reference:
- A customer about to churn over this
- A competitor who just shipped it
- A new technical capability that makes it possible now
- A metric that crossed a threshold

### Success metrics
Metrics must be measurable before you ship. If you can't measure it today, you can't measure success after shipping.

Bad metric: "Users are happier"
Good metric: "Friday standup prep time decreases from `>15min` to `<5min` (measured via survey)"

### Scope
The "out of scope" section is the most valuable part. It documents what you actively decided *not* to build, which prevents scope creep during engineering.

### Open questions
Honest PRDs surface uncertainty. Pretending you know everything creates hidden risks. List questions that, if answered differently, would change the design.

## Exercise

Take the JTBD statement you wrote in Lesson 1 and complete the full one-page PRD template. Share it with someone who wasn't in the room when you wrote the problem statement and ask: "Could you build this?"

If the answer is anything other than an immediate yes, find the gap.
