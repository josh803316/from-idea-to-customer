## The question nobody asks

When engineers debate tech stacks, the conversation usually sounds like this:

> "I've been using Rust for my side projects and it's blazing fast."
> "We're a TypeScript shop, let's just use what we know."
> "Did you see that benchmark where X is 10x faster than Y?"

Nobody asks: **What does the business actually need from this technology in the next 18 months?**

That's the CTO question.

<Aside type="insight">
**CTOs don't pick technology. They pick constraints.** Every stack choice is a bet on which constraints matter most: hiring speed, performance, operational complexity, ecosystem maturity, team familiarity.
</Aside>

## The three-axis framework

Every stack decision sits on three axes:

**1. Team velocity** — How fast can *this* team ship features with this technology?
This is not about the technology's theoretical throughput. It's about your specific team, today.

**2. Operational complexity** — What does it cost to run this in production?
Managed services reduce operational burden. Self-hosted solutions give you control but cost you time. The right answer depends on where you are in the company lifecycle.

**3. Hiring surface** — Who can join this project?
Building in Elixir might be technically optimal, but if you're in a market where 10x more engineers know TypeScript, you're creating a hiring bottleneck that will slow the company down.

## The "boring technology" principle

Dan McKinley's famous essay advocates for choosing boring technology. The argument:

You have a limited budget of innovation tokens. Spend them on the hard problems that are *specific to your business*, not on technology choices that thousands of other companies have already validated.

Using PostgreSQL isn't exciting. But it means every database problem you encounter already has a Stack Overflow answer, a consultant, and a monitoring tool. That's a feature.

**When to break the rule**: When the boring option has a clear, measurable shortcoming for your specific use case. "It's slower" is not enough. "We benchmarked it and it can't handle our P99 latency requirement at our expected load" is enough.

## Case study: why this site uses what it uses

Let's make this concrete. Here's the stack for this very platform, and the reasoning:

| Choice | Why |
|--------|-----|
| Bun | Consistent across all repos; familiar to the team |
| Astro | Content-first; excellent SEO out of the box; lets us add React islands without going full SPA |
| Elysia | Same pattern as our other APIs; good TypeScript ergonomics |
| Neon (PostgreSQL) | Managed Postgres with zero ops overhead; scales to zero in dev |
| Raw SQL (no ORM) | Teaching tool: every query is visible and explainable |
| Tailwind | Team already knows it; zero context-switching |

Notice what's absent from each decision: "it's the best" or "it's the fastest". Every decision is grounded in *this team*, *this moment*, *this problem*.

## Documenting the decision

A stack decision made verbally in a Slack thread is a decision that will be re-litigated in 12 months with no context. Document it.

Use an Architecture Decision Record (ADR):

```markdown
# ADR-001: Use PostgreSQL as primary database

## Status: Accepted

## Context
We need a relational database for user data and progress tracking.
We're a team of 2 engineers who both know PostgreSQL well.

## Decision
Use Neon (managed PostgreSQL) for production, local pg for development.

## Consequences
- Zero database ops burden at this scale
- Familiar query language for the whole team
- If we need Redis or a graph database later, we add it — not replace PostgreSQL
```

Short. Honest about tradeoffs. Reviewable in a year.

## Exercise

Write an ADR for one technology decision in a project you're currently working on. If there are no decisions that need documenting, write one for a decision that was already made — reverse-engineer the context and reasoning.

The goal: someone joining your team 12 months from now should be able to read your ADR and understand *why* the decision was made, not just *what* was decided.
