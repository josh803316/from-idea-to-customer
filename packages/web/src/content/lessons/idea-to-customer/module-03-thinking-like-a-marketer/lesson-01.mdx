## The most misunderstood function

In engineering organizations, marketing is frequently dismissed, misunderstood, or conflated with design. Engineers see the output — the landing page, the blog post, the conference booth — and conclude that marketing is the department that makes things look nice and writes copy.

This misses the entire point.

A marketer's job is to create demand. Everything else — the campaigns, the content, the SEO, the messaging, the brand — is in service of that goal. A marketer who doesn't think about demand generation is the same as a developer who doesn't think about the user's actual problem: technically busy, strategically useless.

When a marketer looks at your square on the checkerboard, they ask: **does this communicate value in a way that creates demand?** Not "does this look good." Not "does this explain the feature." Does it make someone who doesn't know us want to know more — and does it make someone who already knows us want to buy?

<Aside type="insight">
Engineers often think positioning is about describing what a product does. Marketers know that positioning is about why a specific person should care — and specifically, what that person is going to do differently after buying than before. The job is not to explain the product. The job is to make someone see themselves in the story.
</Aside>

## Three things most people confuse

**Positioning** is the strategic foundation. It answers: who is this for, what category does it live in, and why is it better than the alternatives for that person? Positioning is decided, not discovered. It's a choice about which part of the market you're competing for and how.

**Messaging** is how the positioning is expressed in language. Same positioning, different messages for different audiences. The developer audience gets one message. The CFO signing the purchase order gets another. The IT administrator who will manage the implementation gets a third. The positioning doesn't change. The expression of it shifts to match what each audience cares about.

**Branding** is the sum of impressions — visual, verbal, cultural — that create an emotional association with the company or product over time. Branding is the long game. It's not what you say once. It's what people think of when they hear your name after encountering you a hundred times.

Most early-stage startups try to do all three simultaneously with zero resources and end up with an inconsistent mess. The marketer's job at early stage is to make sharp choices: what do we position, for whom, and what message do we lead with? Everything else waits.

## Why engineering decisions are marketing decisions

This is the part engineers find surprising: many technical decisions are marketing decisions. They just don't look like it.

**The API surface.** The quality, consistency, and documentation of your API is a marketing asset. A developer who integrates your API in two hours tells their team about it. A developer who spends a week fighting inconsistent responses tells their team about that too. Developer experience is word-of-mouth marketing for developer products.

**The security posture.** At late-stage SaaS, security certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001) are frequently part of enterprise sales cycles. The CTO who built the architecture made marketing choices when they decided how to handle data residency, audit logging, and access controls. Those choices either accelerate or block deals.

**The performance story.** "10x faster than the alternative" is a positioning statement. Whether it's true, and whether it can be demonstrated under real conditions, is an engineering question. The engineer who built the performance benchmarks and the marketer who uses them in collateral are collaborating on the same square.

**The integration story.** Every native integration — CRM, HRIS, data warehouse — expands the addressable market and reduces integration friction in the sales cycle. Engineers decide which integrations to build. Those decisions directly affect what markets can be sold into.

## The demand generation funnel

Most engineers have some awareness that marketing involves "the funnel." It's worth being concrete about what that means.

**Awareness:** The prospect doesn't know you exist. Demand generation here is about surface area — SEO, content, social, community, partnerships. The question is: when a prospect goes looking for what you do, do they find you? And when they find you, is the first impression coherent with the positioning?

**Consideration:** The prospect knows you exist and is evaluating you against alternatives. Demand generation here is about differentiation — case studies, comparison pages, demos, technical documentation. The question is: when a prospect is deciding between you and three alternatives, do they have enough information to choose correctly (and is that choice favorable to you)?

**Decision:** The prospect has decided they want to buy from someone like you. The remaining question is whether they choose you. Demand generation here merges with sales — proposals, proof of concepts, procurement support. The question is: is there anything getting in the way of a yes?

**Expansion:** The customer has bought. Can you sell them more? Demand generation for expansion is about adoption, success, and the customer's perception of value delivered. Engineers build the product experience that drives expansion. Marketers help customers articulate the value they're receiving (which drives both retention and referrals).

<Aside type="warning">
The funnel model is a simplification. Real buyers don't move linearly through stages. But the model is useful as a diagnostic — if you don't know where in the funnel a specific demand problem lives, you're unlikely to solve it with the right intervention.
</Aside>

## Exercise

Take one feature from a product you've worked on — ideally one you built or were involved in building.

Apply the marketer's lens:

1. **Positioning:** Who specifically is this for? What problem does it solve for them? What would they have done without it?
2. **Messaging:** Write one sentence explaining this feature for a technical user, and one sentence for a non-technical buyer. Are they different? They should be.
3. **Demand creation:** Does this feature make someone who doesn't have it want it? Why? What's the hook?
4. **Engineering-marketing intersection:** Is there anything about the technical implementation that affects the marketing story? (Performance, integration surface, security posture, reliability?)

Conference room exercise: present this feature to the LLM playing your CMO. The CMO needs to understand it well enough to include it in a product launch email. Get the CMO to sign off on the messaging. If they ask questions you can't answer, you've found a gap — in the feature, in the documentation, or in your own understanding of what was built and why.
